Final summary

This study examined whether mean height differs across different levels of milk drinking frequency using the YRBS dataset.
Group variable: NoMilkDrinking (0 = No milk drinking, 1 = Occasional milk drinking, 2 = Frequent milk drinking)
Response variable: Height (HowTallAreYouWithoutShoesInMeters, measured in meters)
Method: One-way ANOVA followed by Tukey HSD post-hoc test
Key results:
The one-way ANOVA showed a significant difference in mean height among the three groups.(F(2, 12665) = 139.0559, p < 0.001).
The null hypothesis (H₀: μ₁ = μ₂ = μ₃) was rejected at α = 0.05.
Descriptively, the highest mean height was observed in the Frequent milk drinking group (1.712 m), followed by the Occasional milk drinking group, and the lowest mean height was in the No milk drinking group (1.675 m).
Tukey HSD post-hoc comparisons confirmed that the Frequent milk drinking group had significantly higher mean height than the other groups, and the Occasional group was also higher than the No milk group.
Conclusion:
There is sufficient statistical evidence at the 0.05 significance level to conclude that mean height differs across milk drinking frequency groups.
Final interpretation:
Frequent milk drinkers tend to be taller on average compared to occasional and non-milk drinkers. The difference in mean height across groups is statistically significant (p < 0.05), indicating an association between milk drinking frequency and height.
However, since the data are observational and self-reported, this result should be interpreted as an association rather than evidence of a causal effect of milk consumption on height.
